Labor and Cost-of-Living Situation — Philippines 2025

The labor and economic rights situation in the Philippines has grown increasingly critical in 2025. Workers continue to struggle with low wages and rising prices for food, housing, transport, and other essentials. Many families report difficulties in meeting their basic needs despite full-time employment. While the government has approved small wage increases in some regions, labor groups insist these are inadequate. In response, workers across the country have organized protests, strikes, and public campaigns demanding fair wages, affordable goods, and permanent employment.

Wage & Living Cost Situation

Workers in most regions earn ₱500–₱700 per day, far below the ₱1,200 daily living wage suggested by labor organizations such as Kilusang Mayo Uno (KMU) and Bukluran ng Manggagawang Pilipino (BMP). Rising prices for food, fuel, utilities, and transportation—increasing 8–10% in 2025—have made it nearly impossible for families to cover their basic needs.

The widening gap between wages and living costs has forced workers to make difficult choices, such as prioritizing food over education or medical care. Many families are reporting debt accumulation and reliance on informal loans, which increases financial insecurity.

Worker Protests and Mobilizations

Worker protests have become a prominent feature of the labor landscape in 2025, with events taking place nationwide. These protests are organized by unions, labor coalitions, and informal worker groups to demand fair wages, relief from inflation, and job security.

Major Protests and Events

Labor Day, May 1, 2025: Thousands of workers, including public transport drivers, factory workers, domestic workers, and service sector employees, marched in Manila, Baguio, Cebu, Davao, and Iloilo. They carried banners reading: “₱1,200 is our right!”, “Stop exploitation—fair wages now!”, and “Prices are rising, why are wages stagnant?”

July 2025 — UWIN Protest: The Unity for Wage Increase Now (UWIN) coalition held demonstrations outside the Department of Labor and Employment (DOLE) in Manila. Protestors criticized the government’s ₱50 wage increase as insufficient and called for national legislation guaranteeing a living wage.

Bonifacio Day Rallies (November 30, 2025): Labor groups organized rallies to commemorate the birth of revolutionary leader Andres Bonifacio, tying historical struggles for workers’ rights to the current fight for fair wages and affordable goods. Protestors emphasized the end of contractualization and demanded secure employment and protection of labor rights.

Protest Tactics and Activities

Marches and demonstrations, placards and visual campaigns, petitions, social media campaigns, and community forums have been employed to educate citizens and amplify worker demands nationwide.

Government and Business Responses

Government Response: DOLE approved minor wage hikes in certain regions, typically ranging from ₱50–₱100 per day, far below workers’ demands. Officials have stated that increases must balance workers’ needs with economic stability, but labor groups argue that small increases fail to address rising living costs.

Business Response: Many MSMEs expressed concern that higher wages may increase operational costs, potentially leading to layoffs or higher prices. Labor advocates counter that fair wages are essential to maintain economic stability and prevent social inequality.

Public Debate: Worker protests have sparked national discussion about wage adequacy, inflation, and the government’s responsibility to protect vulnerable sectors. Media coverage has increased public awareness and pressure on policymakers.

Key Demands from Workers

Labor groups have identified several pressing demands:

  • ₱1,200 daily living wage — to cover basic needs for families.
  • ₱200 legislated wage increase — ensuring wage growth is guaranteed by law.
  • End contractualization (“endo”) — securing permanent jobs with benefits.
  • Lower prices for essential goods — to reduce the burden of inflation.
  • Stronger labor rights enforcement — protecting union activities, collective bargaining, and workers’ advocacy.

Impact of Protests

The protests have highlighted major social and economic issues:

Economic Impact: Exposed the gap between wages and living costs, showing that full-time employment does not guarantee a decent standard of living.

Social Impact: Emphasized dignity, family welfare, and the right to earn a living wage. Protestors shared stories about children missing school, families cutting meals, and medical needs being neglected.

Political Impact: Forced lawmakers, policymakers, and government agencies to respond publicly and consider reforms. Labor movements have strengthened their networks and increased public support.
