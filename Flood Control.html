Flood-Control Corruption and Nationwide Protests in the Philippines

A huge protest erupted in the Philippines after massive corruption in government flood-control projects was uncovered. What started as anger over missing or fake flood-prevention projects escalated into a broader movement, especially among young people, demanding honesty, justice, and major government reforms.

Protests and Violence

In Manila, the protest turned violent. At Ayala Bridge near the Office of the President, over 200 protesters were arrested after throwing rocks, bottles, and homemade firebombs at the police. Riot police responded with force.

The unrest was only part of nationwide outrage. Tens of thousands participated in the “Trillion Peso March” at Luneta Park and the EDSA Shrine, protesting billions of pesos meant for flood-control that were lost, stolen, or misused. Filipino citizens, who suffer from annual floods, believed corruption worsened these disasters.

Protesters demanded: the arrest and punishment of corrupt officials, the return of stolen public funds, and in some cases, even the reinstatement of the death penalty for officials guilty of large-scale plunder. This movement mirrors a wider trend in Asia, where youth in countries like Indonesia, Nepal, and Timor-Leste are protesting government abuse and corruption.

The ‘Pie Sharing’ of Greed

Senator Panfilo Lacson, a former police general and lawmaker, revealed the scale of corruption in flood-control projects during a privilege speech. He described nearly 2 trillion pesos (~NZD 57.1 billion) spent on these projects as failing to protect communities, with over half released in just three years (2023–2025).

Lacson explained a “pie-sharing” system where contracts are divided for kickbacks: DPWH officials take 8–10% of the contract value, bidding committees take 5–6%, and politicians who own the projects take 20–25%. Contractors are left with a tiny portion, forcing them to cut corners or use poor materials, resulting in substandard or ghost projects.

Mayor Benjamin Magalong supported Lacson’s claims with examples of overpricing: rock netting (for landslide protection) cost 17,000 pesos/m² (~NZD 507.6) versus the market price of 6,000 pesos (~NZD 179), and reflective lane dividers (“cat eyes”) cost 11,720.69 pesos each (~NZD 349.66) versus 1,624 pesos (~NZD 48.45) in the market.

Both Lacson and Magalong concluded that contractors receive only a small portion of the budget, while the majority of stolen funds go to lawmakers in the House and Senate.

The Anatomy of Budget Manipulation

The issue intensified after President Ferdinand Marcos Jr.’s State of the Nation Address on July 18, where he reprimanded officials and disclosed DPWH’s preliminary findings: over 500 billion pesos were allocated for flood-control projects, but 100 billion pesos went to only 15 contractors.

GMA News detailed the budget manipulation process. It starts with the National Expenditure Program (NEP), where politicians, DPWH officials, and contractors allegedly hide projects within the official budget. The real manipulation occurs later, as a small committee in the House of Representatives can modify the budget. Insertions also happen in the Bicameral Conference Committee stage and through Unprogrammed Appropriations (UA) in the final General Appropriations Act (GAA).

The final DPWH budget for 2025 ballooned from an initial 900 billion pesos to over 1 trillion pesos. Fake bidding and pre-selected contractors meant that sometimes only 30% of funds were used to build projects, leading to ghost projects or poor-quality structures.

Government Response

President Marcos Jr. responded by forming an independent commission, firing the DPWH secretary, and removing suspicious projects from the 2025 national budget. Funds were reallocated to health and education. Justice Secretary Crispin Remulla announced asset freezes on officials under investigation, though only Bulacan officials were publicly named.

Allegations also touched the President’s family, with former House Speaker Martin Romualdez accused of involvement in budget insertions. Despite his resignation, the public remains skeptical about whether powerful allies will face accountability.

International scrutiny included reports that South Korean President Lee Jae-myung halted a 28-billion-peso loan over corruption concerns, though the Philippine Department of Finance denied the loan existed.
