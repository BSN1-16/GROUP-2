The worsening rice crisis in the Philippines has intensified public outrage, prompting protests led by farmers, consumer groups, and food security advocates at the Department of Agriculture in Quezon City. Demonstrators criticized government interventions such as the 60-day suspension of rice imports and tariff adjustments, describing them as temporary solutions that do not address structural issues in the rice sector.

These protests, held on August 13, 2025, drew attention to the growing disparity between retail rice prices and plummeting farmgate prices, highlighting the economic hardship faced by rural communities.

Palay Prices Plummet

Farmgate prices of palay have reportedly dropped to P8 to P10 per kilo, leaving many rice farmers struggling to recover production costs. Many producers now face losses that threaten their ability to sustain farming operations.

"The farmgate prices we are seeing now barely cover production costs. This is not just a minor setback it threatens the very livelihood of millions of farmers across the country," said Cathy Estavillo, secretary general of the Amihan National Federation of Peasant Women.

Criticism of Government Programs

Protesters criticized initiatives such as KADIWA Centers, Executive Order 62 on tariff adjustments, the Rice for All Program, Sulit and Nutri Rice programs, the Maximum Suggested Retail Price, and the BBM Benteng Bigas initiative. These programs were described as temporary relief measures, failing to address the structural causes of low palay prices.

Call to Repeal the Rice Liberalization Law

Protesters renewed calls for the repeal of Republic Act 11203, also known as the Rice Liberalization Law. They argue that the law allows unlimited rice imports, which depress local prices, drive farmers into debt, and hinder national rice self-sufficiency.

Holding Leaders Accountable

In addition to criticizing specific programs, protesters held President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. accountable for the ongoing rice crisis. They emphasized the government's failure to implement structural reforms and called for policies that genuinely support rice farmers.

Nationwide Protests and Analysis

The Quezon City protest is part of a series of nationwide actions highlighting the widening gap between production costs and market prices. Analysts argue that the crisis reflects broader structural problems in agriculture, including inadequate infrastructure, insufficient support programs, and effects of liberalized rice imports under RA 11203.

While officials acknowledge challenges, many farmers remain skeptical. Temporary measures are deemed insufficient; only comprehensive reforms can ensure sustainable rice production and food security.
